Block 1 · Minutes 0–50
The Basic Tool: A Good Translation
기본 도구: 좋은 번역본
⏱ 0:00 — The Linguist's Dilemma (10 min)
Let me begin with a confession that may disturb you — and should. Every time you open your English Bible, you are not reading the Bible. You are reading a translation. And every translation is already an interpretation.
The sixty-six books were composed in three languages: Hebrew, Aramaic, and Greek. Unless you have studied all three, you have never read a single verse of Scripture in its original form.
Write on the board: 'Every translation is an interpretation.' Allow a moment for that to land.
I. The Three Languages of the Biblical Text
성경의 세 언어
Biblical Hebrew — a Semitic language of striking concreteness, rooted in images of movement, body, and land. Its verbs are the backbone of the sentence; its nouns carry relational weight.
Aramaic — the international lingua franca of the ancient Near East. Portions of Ezra and Daniel were written in Aramaic; Jesus likely spoke it in daily life.
Koine Greek — the common Greek of the marketplace, spread by Alexander the Great's conquests. Not the elegant Greek of Plato — simpler, more flexible, the Greek of ordinary people writing with apostolic urgency.
II. What a Translator Must Decide
번역자가 결정해야 하는 것들
Fee and Stuart illustrate with 1 Corinthians 7:36. Four major English translations render this verse with four different social scenarios. Is Paul addressing a fiancée, a daughter, or a celibate partner? The Greek is genuinely ambiguous — but Paul was not.
Case Study · 1 Cor 7:36
Four translations — four different social scenarios. Same Greek text. What does this tell us?
NKJV
"If any man thinks that he is behaving improperly toward his virgin…"
NASB
"If any man thinks that he is acting unbecomingly toward his virgin daughter…"
NIV
"If anyone is worried that he might not be acting honorably toward the virgin he is engaged to…"
NEB
"If a man has a partner in celibacy and feels that he is not behaving properly towards her…"
Interpretive implication: Where translations agree, the interpreter is on solid ground. Where they diverge, a genuine difficulty in the original text is surfaced. That divergence is your invitation to dig deeper — not a crisis, but a hermeneutical flag.
III. A Taxonomy of English Translations
번역본의 세 가지 유형
⏱ 0:20 — Translation Philosophy (15 min)
A. Formal Equivalence · NASB, ESV, NKJV
Formal equivalence attempts to render the original as literally as possible — preserving word order and grammatical structures wherever English allows. Invaluable for close study. But translating idioms word-for-word can produce nonsense or false precision.
B. Dynamic Equivalence · NIV, NLT
Dynamic equivalence asks: what would this sound like in natural modern English? More readable and captures original impact — but makes more interpretive decisions. The NIV renders σάρξ ('flesh') as 'sinful nature' in Rom 7:18 — one interpretation among several, invisible to the English reader.
C. Paraphrase · The Message, The Living Bible
Paraphrases are expansive, interpretive retellings. Valuable for devotional reading — never use as a primary study text. The Message is Peterson's interpretation, not a translation in the technical sense.
IV. Korean Linguistic Bridge
개역개정, 새번역, 현대인의 성경
Korean translations — 개역개정, 새번역, 현대인의 성경 — each carry their own philosophies. Korean's verb-final structure aligns more naturally with Hebrew and Greek in some respects. Korean honorific levels require constant decisions about relational posture. A bilingual reader has a richer interpretive resource.
Block 2 · Minutes 60–110
The Hermeneutical Spiral
해석학적 나선: 이해의 형태
I. A Metaphor That Changes Everything
닫힌 원 대 나선형
⏱ 1:00 — The Shape of Understanding (12 min)
Osborne opens with a gold coins image: imagine gold discovered on the church's back lot — increasing in number with every foot of depth. How quickly would you start digging? The deeper you go, the greater the rewards. This is the reality of Bible study.
Biblical interpretation is not a closed circle but a spiral. A closed circle implies interpretation is self-referential — we bring our preunderstanding and the text confirms it. The spiral corrects this: it moves forward, converging toward the text's intended meaning with each pass.
It is not a closed circle that can never detect the true meaning but [I] am spiraling nearer and nearer to the text's intended meaning as I refine my hypotheses and allow the text to continue to challenge and correct those alternative interpretations.
— Grant Osborne
II. Three Dimensions of Hermeneutics
과학, 예술, 영적 행위
⏱ 1:12 — Science, Art, Spirit (8 min)
A science — providing logical, orderly classification of interpretive laws. Some interpretations are demonstrably better than others.
An art — an acquired skill demanding imagination and practice. Rules can be learned without mastery; the art develops through thousands of hours of encounter with the text.
A spiritual act — uniquely dependent on the illuminating work of the Holy Spirit. Human effort is necessary and never sufficient. The doctrine of illumination presupposes rigorous study — it does not replace it.
III. The Four Distances
리쾨르의 거리화 간격
⏱ 1:20 — Distanciation Gap (10 min)
The philosopher Paul Ricoeur speaks of the distanciation gap between the biblical text and contemporary readers. The question is not whether the gap exists — it does — but whether it is bridgeable. Klein, Blomberg, and Hubbard identify four areas:
Case Study · The Four Distances
Amos 2:6 — "those who sell the righteous for silver, and the needy for a pair of sandals." How does each distance shape our understanding?
Time
8th century B.C. Israel under Jeroboam II — 2,800 years separate us from this text's original moment.
Culture
Debt slavery, patron-client economics, and legal corruption in the Israelite gate-court are not abstract — they are the precise social machinery Amos dissects.
Geography
Amos is a shepherd from Tekoa (Judah) delivering oracles to the prosperous north (Israel) — a geographical outsider speaking judgment into the center.
Language
'A pair of sandals' is a legal idiom for a trifling sum in property transactions. Without Hebrew idiom awareness, the verse sounds trivial. With it, the moral outrage is clear.
IV. Meaning and Significance
허쉬의 구분
⏱ 1:30 — Hirsch's Distinction (8 min)
Case Study · Romans 6:23
"The wages of sin is death, but the gift of God is eternal life in Christ Jesus our Lord." — Romans 6:23
Meaning
What Paul intended in 58 A.D. — singular, stable, determined at the moment of writing. Does not change whether read in Rome, Seoul, or 2026.
Significance
For a martyr in 2nd-century Rome: defiance of imperial power. For a Korean seminary student in 2026: vocational orientation. For a person in addiction crisis: immediate pastoral address. Same meaning — vastly different significance.
The danger: confusing significance with meaning — treating what a text means for me today as though it were what the author actually said. This collapses the distinction and undermines the text's authority.
A text cannot mean what it could never have meant for its original readers/hearers. The true meaning of the biblical text for us is what God originally intended it to mean when it was first spoken or written.
— Fee & Stuart
V. Context — The Most Important Word
역사적 문맥과 논리적 문맥
⏱ 1:38 — Context: Historical and Logical (22 min)
Osborne: if a student answers 'context' to any question, there's a 50% chance of being right. Words do not mean things in isolation — they mean things in sentences, paragraphs, books, and historical settings. Osborne distinguishes two dimensions:
Historical context — the author's identity, date, audience circumstances, and purpose. Knowing Amos was a Tekoa shepherd addressing prosperous but unjust northern Israel transforms his words from platitude into surgical social diagnosis.
Logical context — the structural and literary relationship around a passage: immediate sentences, section argument, book structure, and canonical context. Visualized as concentric circles. The immediate context is the final arbiter for word meaning decisions.
Charting a book — a macro-level structural analysis before engaging any individual passage. Read the entire book in one sitting; identify major divisions; note the flow of thought. Without this, even brilliant micro-level exegesis can go wrong.
Block 3 · Minutes 120–170
Grammar, Text Criticism, and the Surface of the Text
문법, 본문 비평, 그리고 텍스트의 표면
I. Exegesis and Eisegesis
주해와 삽입 해석
⏱ 2:00 — The Fundamental Distinction (5 min)
Exegesis
From Greek ἐξήγησις — 'to lead out of.' Reading meaning out of the text — drawing out what the author actually put there.
Eisegesis
From Greek εἰσ — 'into.' Reading meaning into the text — projecting what you wish were there, what tradition says, or what supports your conclusion.
Every interpreter commits eisegesis constantly and unavoidably. Good hermeneutics names the filters, holds them loosely, and allows the text to interrogate and reform them. This is why the spiral is the right metaphor: the text pushes back; we revise; we return.
II. Textual Criticism
사본 전통과 본문 비평의 원칙들
⏱ 2:05 — Text Criticism (20 min)
We do not possess original autographs. NT: over 5,700 Greek manuscripts. OT traditions: Masoretic Text, Septuagint (LXX), Samaritan Pentateuch, and Dead Sea Scrolls (1947, pushing oldest Hebrew manuscripts back ~1,000 years).
Case Study · Mark 16:9–20
The longer ending of Mark is present in the KJV, absent or bracketed in virtually all modern translations. Why?
Evidence
Codex Sinaiticus and Codex Vaticanus — the oldest and most reliable Greek manuscripts — end at 16:8: "for they were afraid."
Style
The vocabulary and style of vv. 9–20 differ markedly from Mark's usage elsewhere in the Gospel. A later editor appears to have compiled resurrection-appearance traditions.
Response
We neither panic nor pretend it isn't there. We acknowledge it honestly. None of the doctrines it affirms depend on these verses — they are all well attested elsewhere in the NT.
Text-critical principle: External criteria (manuscript age + distribution) and internal criteria (style, difficulty, length) must be weighed together. The harder reading is often more likely to be original — scribes tended to expand, not simplify.
III. Grammatical Analysis
그리스어 시제, 법, 태
⏱ 2:25 — Grammar and Meaning (25 min)
Grammar is the architectural blueprint of communication. Word meaning is not self-contained — words mean things only in grammatical context. The word δικαιόω (dikaioo) can mean 'to declare righteous,' 'to acquit,' or 'to vindicate' depending on who is doing what to whom.
Case Study · 1 John 3:9 · Greek Present Tense
"No one who is born of God will continue to sin… they cannot go on sinning, because they have been born of God." — 1 John 3:9 (NIV)
Grammar
'Continue to sin' and 'go on sinning' reflect Greek present tense forms — indicating ongoing, habitual action, not occasional acts.
Meaning
John is not saying believers never sin. He is saying that sinning as a characteristic lifestyle is incompatible with being born of God.
Impact
This grammatical distinction changes the entire interpretation — from an impossible demand (never sin) to a description of regenerate character (sin no longer dominates).
Case Study · Phil 2:6 · The Participial Question
"Who, being in very nature God, did not consider equality with God something to be used to his own advantage." — Philippians 2:6 (NIV)
Participle
ὑπάρχων (hyparchon) — the Greek participle is grammatically ambiguous between concessive, causal, and temporal readings.
Concessive
"Although he was in the form of God" — emphasis on the paradox of the Incarnation; his divine nature did not prevent the humiliation.
Causal
"Because he was in the form of God" — the Incarnation flows from his divine character; who he is determines how he acts.
Stakes
The translation you choose has significant Christological implications. This is not a secondary textual matter — it is the heart of the passage.
IV. What If You Don't Know the Languages?
원어 없이 문법 활용하기
Memorize the Hebrew and Greek alphabets. This opens interlinear Bibles, analytical concordances, and a massive range of language resources.
Invest in Bible software. Logos, Accordance, BibleHub, or Blue Letter Bible allow you to hover over an English word and see the original term, its lexical range, grammatical form, and usage elsewhere.
Use multiple translations as a grammatical tool. Where translations diverge sharply, you are looking at genuine grammatical ambiguity in the original. Treat those divergences as flags.

Discussion Questions
Q1
Fee and Stuart argue that using only one Bible translation commits you to the exegetical decisions of that committee. How does this challenge the way you have read the Bible in the past?
Q2
Osborne describes hermeneutics as simultaneously a science, an art, and a spiritual act. Which dimension is most developed in your practice? Which most underdeveloped?
Q3
The four distances are real — but bridgeable. What resources and practices do you have right now for bridging each of them?
Q4
Hirsch's distinction between meaning and significance is central to Osborne's project. How does this distinction change the way you think about 'applying' a biblical text?
Q5
Korean question: How does reading in Korean (개역개정 or 새번역) alongside English change or deepen your interpretation? Give a specific example.
Q6
Mark 16:9–20 and the 1 John 3:9 tense issue both illustrate how textual and grammatical decisions change interpretation. Which case study surprised you most — and why?
